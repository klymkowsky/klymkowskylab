While it is clear that handedness is partly genetically determined [see Annett 1973], it is certainly not due to simple genetic factors (like those that Mendel studied).  Rather a number of genes and environmental factors interact to determine handedness. 

These factors not only lead to left-handedness, but also to changes in the organization of the brain.

In general, said Dr. Geschwind, left-handers have less asymmetric brains, with more even distribution over the two hemispheres. “Perhaps a more accurate conceptual way to think about them is as non-right-handers,” he said. “Many of them are much more likely to be ambidextrous and have fine motor abilities with their right hands.”

The brain, in turn can influence the behavior of the immune system [see Neveu 1992].  While I am no expert in this area, these immune system effects appear to be quite minor (if they exist at all) and very difficult to study. 

There are some studies that left-handed people have shorter average life spans than right-handed people, [see Coren 1994]. What is relevant to your question, however, is why. 

It has been suggested that lefthanders are more prone to accidents, and a study of cricket players suggested that lefthanders were more likely to die of "accidents or in warfare" [see Aggleton et al., 1993].

There is NO evidence that the difference in handedness is due to "heart strain", so the absence of limbs would not increase life-span, and given that limbs are important for the normal functioning of the body, might well decrease it.   

One interesting fact to note is that handedness can be different between different "great ape" species (which includes humans).  While chimpanzees, bonobos, and gorillas are primarily right handed, orangutans appear to be primarily left-handed [see Hopkins et al., 2011].
