You are exactly right.  In vertebrates that develop outside or independently of the mother, nutrients are stored as yolk, which consists in large measure of vitellogenin proteins.  These are deposited during oogenesis and are available to the developing embryo.

In eutherian “placental” mammals (such as mice and humans), the mature oocyte, fertilized egg and early embryo have little in the way of stored nutrients.  Within a few cell divisions, which involve reduced cell size, not growth, the embryo becomes dependent on nutrients, first from fluids secreted by the mother and then through nutrients obtained through interactions with the forming placenta. 

From an evolutionary prospective, this change is associated with a loss of the genes encoding vitellogenin proteins in the lineage leading to eutherian mammals (represented by humans and dogs).  Vitellogenin genes are still present in marsupials and monotremes (like the platypus). [see Brawand et al., 2008]
