Desmoplakin in rat cardiomyocytes
 
Our gallery of stained rat neonatal cardiomyocytes:

desmoplakin plectin desmin
In the course of our studies we have characterized the distribution of a number of cytoskeletal and adherence junction proteins in rat cardiomyocytes.  Many of these proteins will be focus of future studies, but here we simply present some representative images.

 Deconvolution microscopy:  Typical light microscopic images are degraded by out of focus information arising above and below the focal plane of the objective lens.  This information can be removed either during the course of recording the image, through the use of a confocal microscope (the animation at the top of the page is a series of confocal images), or computationally.  Both techniques lead to a sharper, often easier to analyze image.   Because of the structural complexity of cardiomyocytes, confocal/deconvolution microscopy are required to generate a clear image of the cells, and the effects of various experimental manipulations.
    To this end, we have begun to use a 2D-deconvoluting microscope system designed by  Intelligent Imaging Innovations, Inc. (and bankrolled by  Jackie Lee & Brad Olwin of our department).  Examples of images obtained by this scope are illustrated below .
Desmin filaments can be clearly seen running between Z-lines in the deconvoluted image 
(at least if you look closely)
 
Using deconvolution, we should be able to construct a three-dimensional map of plectin organization in  cardiomyocyte, and to characterize the effects of disrupting that system using mutated forms of desmin, as well as various anti-plectin polypeptides that we have developed. 
 

Conventional Light microscopic images (very convoluted!)
 
 a-actinin: 
notice the high degree of coordination between the contractile systems of the two cells.
 
connexin 43:   connexins, the structural subunits of gap junctions, play a critical role in coordination of heart muscle contraction.
 
 
 p120ctn: 
one of the catenins (cadherin-binding proteins), p120 is concentrated a sites of cell-cell adhesion. 
 
a-catenin: 
this catenin, which mediates interactions with actin filaments, is present both at the intercalated discs, and associated with the Z-lines. 
 
desmin:
the muscle specific intermediate filament protein desmin is associated with the periphery of the Z-disc, and appears to connect Z-lines longitudinally.  In cells that interact, desmin is also concentrated at the intercalated disc. 
 
 
 
desmoplakin:
a desmosomal catenin, desmoplakin is concentrated at the intercalated disc.  We also find it decorating the Z-discs, perhaps through an interaction with desmin and plectin. 
 
plectin:
is found at both the intercalated disc and associated with Z-lines,  much like a-catenin.  Plectin is known to interact with intermediate filaments and actin, serving as a linker between these two cytoskeletal systems.  Mutations in plectin have been linked to human muscular dystrophy, and mice deficient in plectin display defects in heart muscle structure.
